# Persona Grill Questions + Anti-Generic Guardrails

The decision tree for extracting behaviorally distinct user types. Ask ONE question per turn with a recommended answer attached. Reject generic answers. Explore the product before asking anything the material already answers.

## Contents
- How to reject a generic answer

## How to Reject a Generic Answer

The failure mode is "everyone is our user." It describes no one and gives the critiques nothing to test. When you get a vague or all-encompassing answer, push:

- **Force a primary:** "If only one of these people could succeed, who has to? Whose failure means the product failed?"
- **Force behavior over demographics:** "'25–40 professionals' tells me nothing about how they use it. What are they trying to *do*, how often, and under what pressure?"
- **Force a real difference:** "You named two types — but would they use this differently or want different things? If not, they're one type."
- **Ground in reality:** "Is that who actually uses it today, or who you wish used it? Critique the product for real users first."

Keep pushing until the answer would change which scenario a critic walks or how they'd weight priorities.

## The Question Tree

Ask in this order. Each turn: one question + a recommended answer + a one-line reason.

1. **What the app does** — In one line, what is the core job the app does for people? (Ground truth for every persona.)
2. **Primary user** — Who is the one user who *must* succeed — the person the product exists for? (Exactly one primary. Reject "everyone.")
3. **Distinct secondary types** — Is there anyone else who uses it with a *different* goal or in a *different* way? Name them only if they'd behave differently. (Multi-persona, but only real distinctions.)
4. **Role & context of use** (per type) — Who are they in relation to the app, and where/when/how do they use it? Device, environment, frequency, and the stakes if they get it wrong.
5. **Proficiency** (per type) — How skilled are they with this *kind* of tool (tech), and with the *subject matter* (domain)? Novice/intermediate/expert on each — they can differ.
6. **Goal / job-to-be-done** (per type) — What specific outcome are they trying to reach, and what does "done" look like? (This becomes a critique scenario.)
7. **Motivations / what they care about** (per type) — What matters most to them — speed, control, safety, confidence, delight, cost, trust? (This becomes the UX priority lens.)
8. **Frustrations / bail conditions** (per type) — What makes them give up, distrust it, or rage-quit? Where do similar tools lose them?
9. **"This is for me" cue** (per type) — What would make this person feel the product was clearly built for them — in tone, density, language, or feel? (This becomes the design resonance test.)
10. **Anti-users** — Who is this explicitly *not* for? Whose needs will you deliberately not serve? (Clarifies the edges.)
11. **Priority / weight** — Rank the types. Which one wins when their needs conflict?

Stop when the primary and any genuinely distinct types have a concrete goal (6), a priority lens (7), and a resonance cue (9).

## Anti-Generic Guardrails

Flag and push back when an answer drifts toward these. Accept them only if the user insists after being challenged.

| Generic drift | Push toward |
|---------------|-------------|
| "Everyone" / "all users" / "anyone who…" | A single primary user who must succeed |
| Demographics only ("18–65", "professionals") | Behavior: goals, frequency, context, pressure |
| Two types that behave identically | Merge into one, or find the real difference |
| Aspirational users who don't actually use it | The real current users first; note aspirational separately |
| A job title with no goal ("managers") | The outcome that title is trying to achieve |
| "Power users and beginners" with no specifics | What each actually does differently, and which is primary |
| Persona with no frustrations or success state | The bail condition and the observable "done" |

If, after grilling, the set is still one undifferentiated blob or a pile of interchangeable types, say so directly and re-open the primary-user question. A sharp primary plus honest anti-users beats five vague personas.
